The Rise of Consumerism: How social media is Shaping Our Desires

Over the past few decades, consumerism has seen a remarkable shift. What was once an occasional trip to the store or a carefully planned purchase has now become an almost daily experience. The growth in consumerism isn’t just about the increase in what we buy; it’s about how we buy and why. A major player in this shift is social media, which has not only altered the way we shop but also the way we view our consumption habits.

Consumerology, a term popularized by Philip Graves in his book Consumer.ology, refers to the study of consumer behavior and decision-making. While not an official academic discipline, it highlights the growing interest in understanding what drives people to make purchasing decisions. This field explores the various influences on consumer choices, from advertising to social pressure, and reveals how the line between need and desire is increasingly blurred. In today’s world, the push to consume has never been stronger, making the study of these behaviors more relevant than ever.

The explosion of consumerism can be linked to a variety of factors: globalization, increased access to products through e-commerce, and technological advancements, to name a few. However, the rapid growth in consumerism is also fuelled by the more subtle influences of our daily lives, particularly through social media. Social platforms like Instagram, TikTok, and Facebook have become a digital marketplace where brands, influencers, and even our friends are showcasing the latest must-have products. We’re constantly exposed to ads, curated content, and viral trends that create a sense of urgency to consume, often for items we didn’t even know we wanted.

Social media platforms are now playing a crucial role in shaping what we want and, often, when we want it. Take a scroll through your feed, and you’re bound to come across sponsored posts, influencer recommendations, or a viral challenge showcasing the latest fashion trends or gadgets. The appeal of instant gratification is built right into these platforms, whether it's through one-click shopping links or the endless stream of curated content making us feel like we're missing out if we don’t join the hype. What social media has done is tap into the psychology of FOMO (Fear of Missing Out). The constant influx of new products and services, coupled with the fear of being left behind, has led people to consume more than ever before. It’s no longer just about purchasing items, it’s about buying into a lifestyle or social identity that’s being presented to us online.

Social media has shifted how we perceive consumption. The old idea of purchasing something for its utility has evolved into purchasing for its status or social value. It’s not just about owning a new product, it’s about how that product can enhance our social image or give us a sense of belonging. What’s interesting is that many of these trends are cyclical. From fast fashion to different cosmetic products, it’s not just the consumer demand that has evolved but also the mindset of consumers. Instead of focusing on long-term satisfaction, social media has emphasized instant trends, quick fixes, and fleeting desires. This change in how we view value, from longevity to trendiness, has changed the way brands market their products, too.

Social media isn't only about passive consumption, it often creates pressure to actively consume more. Influencers and celebrities portray idealized lifestyles that seem within reach but come with a catch: a constant flow of consumption. You can spot the newest tech, latest fashion, or the hottest restaurant opening, all through a quick glance at an influencer’s story or feed. This creates a subtle but powerful force that makes us feel as though we need these items to maintain or enhance our sense of identity.

While it’s easy to get caught up in the wave of consumerism, it’s worth considering the environmental and social implications. The endless cycle of buying, using, and discarding creates a strain on our resources and contributes to waste. The challenge moving forward is finding balance, embracing the convenience and creativity that social media brings while being mindful of the long-term impact of our consumption habits.

In a world where we’re always connected and constantly influenced by what we see online, it’s no wonder that consumerism has grown so rapidly. Social media plays a central role in shaping our desires, encouraging not only the act of purchasing but the very perception of what we should buy. As the lines between need and want become more blurred, it’s up to us as consumers to stay aware of the impact our choices have, not just on our wallets, but on the world around us. Ultimately, the growth of consumerism isn’t just about increased consumption, it’s about understanding how social media has reshaped our desires and how those desires shape our world.
